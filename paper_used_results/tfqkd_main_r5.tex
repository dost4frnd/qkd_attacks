\begin{abstract}
The global transition toward quantum-secure communications is accelerating in response to the imminent threat posed by fault-tolerant quantum computers to classical public-key cryptography. Quantum Key Distribution (QKD), particularly Twin-Field QKD (TF-QKD), provides theoretically unconditional security while overcoming the fundamental rate-distance bounds of traditional optical channels. However, the practical implementation of TF-QKD requires extensive phase stabilization, typically achieved via an optical phase-locked loop (OPLL). This stabilization apparatus introduces profound physical-layer vulnerabilities, such as reference-light tampering, wavelength-switching attacks, and asymmetric channel manipulation. In this work, we provide an exhaustive, mathematically rigorous evaluation of the efficacy of machine learning models in continuously monitoring TF-QKD telemetry data to detect these complex, physical-layer attacks. Utilizing massive, highly structured simulated datasets, the analysis explores the capacity of Quantum Long Short-Term Memory (QLSTM) networks, classical Transformers, and Autoencoders to identify anomalous telemetry. By strictly segregating supervised classification from one-class anomaly detection, we demonstrate a critical vulnerability in classical architectures: while highly-parameterized Transformers achieve perfect memorization in pristine environments, they suffer catastrophic domain-shift failure under asymmetric optical loss. In stark contrast, the proposed Hybrid QLSTM—constrained by the unitarity of quantum rotational gates—naturally enforces robust spectral regularization, maintaining highly stable generalization across operational degradation. Furthermore, the expansive mathematical topology of the quantum Hilbert space inherently isolates unseen zero-day vulnerabilities, generating distinctly separable entropy bounds for unknown attacks that vastly outperform classical models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The global digital economy and the corresponding cybersecurity infrastructure are fundamentally anchored in the computational intractability of mathematical problems, most notably integer factorization and the discrete logarithm problem. These mathematical paradigms form the foundational basis of the Rivest-Shamir-Adleman (RSA) algorithm and Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC) protocols. However, the rapid and accelerating progression of quantum computing technology—specifically the ongoing development of scalable, fault-tolerant quantum processors capable of executing Shor's algorithm—guarantees the polynomial-time circumvention of these classical cryptographic algorithms. To preempt a catastrophic collapse of global secure communications, researchers and network operators are aggressively transitioning toward information-theoretic secure frameworks, primarily Quantum Key Distribution (QKD). QKD theoretically guarantees unconditional security for cryptographic key exchange, anchored not in computational complexity, but in the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics: the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle and the quantum no-cloning theorem.

Historically, practical QKD systems have been rigidly constrained by transmission channel loss, rendering the secret key rate geometrically dependent on the transmission distance. The Pirandola-Laurenza-Ottaviani-Bianchi (PLOB) bound definitively establishes the maximum achievable secret key capacity for any repeaterless QKD protocol over a lossy quantum channel, dictating that the rate scales linearly as $O(\eta)$, where $\eta$ represents the channel transmittance. Twin-Field QKD (TF-QKD) \cite{tfqkd} emerged as a groundbreaking, revolutionary protocol capable of surpassing this fundamental physical constraint without the physical integration of quantum repeaters. By requiring two remote parties (conventionally named Alice and Bob) to transmit optical fields to a central, untrusted intermediate station (Charlie) for single-photon interference, TF-QKD achieves a key rate scaling of $O(\sqrt{\eta})$. Recent experimental milestones have unequivocally demonstrated the viability of TF-QKD over standardized fiber optic distances exceeding 1000 kilometers.

Despite its robust theoretical security proofs and its resilience against detector-side vulnerabilities—inheriting the core advantages of Measurement-Device-Independent QKD (MDI-QKD) \cite{mdi}—the physical realization of TF-QKD introduces profound and unprecedented engineering complexity. The successful execution of the protocol demands absolute phase coherence between the independent optical fields originating from Alice and Bob upon their arrival at Charlie's central beam splitter. This synchronization is typically managed via an optical phase-locked loop (OPLL). However, utilizing a shared, continuous-wave reference light to synchronize frequencies and phases opens entirely new physical attack vectors. Eavesdroppers can dynamically manipulate the calibration routines or the reference fields to compromise the quantum state preparation without triggering conventional Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) alarms.

This report provides an exhaustive, mathematically rigorous evaluation of the efficacy of machine learning models in continuously monitoring TF-QKD telemetry data to detect these complex, physical-layer attacks. Utilizing massive, highly structured simulated datasets designed to emulate baseline, drifting, asymmetric, and unknown threat environments, the analysis explores the capacity of Quantum Long Short-Term Memory (QLSTM) networks, classical LSTMs, Transformers, and unsupervised Autoencoders to identify anomalous telemetry signatures. By intentionally separating supervised classification from one-class anomaly detection, this evaluation rectifies a persistent methodological flaw in security analytics, ensuring that models evaluated on recognized threat signatures are never conflated with systems engineered to detect zero-day exploits. Cross-domain robustness emerges as the major analytical claim, deeply investigating how models trained on pristine, clean telemetry generalize to operationally degraded and asymmetric fiber environments, ultimately demonstrating the superior spectral regularization capabilities of parameterized quantum circuits in the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era.

\section{Background and Literature Review}

\subsection{Evolution of QKD Protocols}
The fundamental premise of QKD is to enable two parties, Alice and Bob, to establish a shared, secret cryptographic key bounded by the unalterable laws of quantum mechanics. The BB84 protocol \cite{bb84} relies on the transmission of single photons encoded in two conjugate bases (e.g., rectilinear and diagonal polarization). Any attempt by an eavesdropper to measure the state in transit induces a wavefunction collapse, generating a detectable increase in the Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER). If the QBER surpasses a predefined threshold (theoretically $\sim 11\%$), the protocol aborts, guaranteeing that the distilled key is secure.

However, true single-photon sources are technologically challenging to implement at scale. Practical systems rely on highly attenuated lasers emitting Weak Coherent Pulses (WCPs). Because WCPs follow a Poisson photon-number distribution, there is a non-zero probability of emitting multiple photons in a single pulse. This loophole facilitates the Photon Number Splitting (PNS) attack \cite{pns}, where Eve blocks single-photon pulses and splits multi-photon pulses, keeping one photon in a quantum memory and forwarding the other to Bob. To combat this, Hwang \cite{hwang} and Lo et al. \cite{lo2005} introduced Decoy-State QKD, which intentionally intersperses pulses of varying intensities to measure the channel yield and tightly bound the behavior of single-photon contributions.

While Decoy-State protocols secure the source, the detectors remain vulnerable. MDI-QKD \cite{mdi} was proposed to eliminate all detector side-channels. In MDI-QKD, Alice and Bob both send WCPs to an untrusted central relay (Charlie) who performs a Bell-State Measurement (BSM). A successful BSM projects Alice and Bob's photons into an entangled state, post-selecting the key correlations. Despite its high security, MDI-QKD is strictly bounded by the channel transmittance $\eta$, scaling as $O(\eta)$. Twin-Field QKD (TF-QKD) \cite{tfqkd} revolutionized this limit by requiring single-photon interference at the central node rather than two-photon interference. By utilizing phase-randomized coherent states and actively tracking the phase difference over the channels, TF-QKD achieves a key rate that scales with $O(\sqrt{\eta})$, effectively doubling the practical transmission distance and making inter-city quantum networks feasible without quantum repeaters.

\subsection{Physical Layer Hardware Vulnerabilities}
The requirement for extremely precise phase stability and timing synchronization in TF-QKD introduces a massive telemetry footprint, simultaneously opening new vectors for physical-layer exploitation. As physical bounds extend, attackers inevitably pivot toward the optical and optoelectronic components required to execute the protocol.

Lydersen et al. \cite{lydersen} and Makarov \cite{makarov} demonstrated a profound vulnerability known as Detector Blinding. Avalanche Photodiodes (APDs) used in QKD operate in Geiger mode, requiring a bias voltage above the breakdown threshold to achieve single-photon sensitivity. By injecting continuous bright light into the fiber, an eavesdropper forces the APD to draw a high current, causing a voltage drop across the bias resistor. This lowers the APD voltage below breakdown, shifting it into the linear mode. Once blinded, the detector only responds to classical, tailored bright pulses sent by Eve.

Another critical vector involves Trojan-Horse Attacks. Gisin et al. \cite{gisin} identified that active optical components, such as phase modulators (PMs) and intensity modulators (IMs), reflect a small fraction of incoming light. Eve can inject bright probe pulses into Alice's or Bob's devices. The back-reflected light picks up the phase or intensity modulation applied to the legitimate quantum state, allowing Eve to read the encoding choice directly.

Furthermore, practical QKD systems often share optical fibers with classical data channels via Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM). Eve can exploit the wavelength-dependent properties of optical components, like beam splitters and fiber dispersion. By slightly shifting the wavelength of the injected photons, she can alter the arrival time or the splitting ratio, facilitating time-shift attacks that bias the detection probabilities at Bob's end, as originally outlined by Jain et al. \cite{jain}.

Beyond these foundational attacks, recent literature has exposed severe OPLL-specific vulnerabilities unique to TF-QKD. The wavelength-switching attack specifically exploits vulnerabilities in the acousto-optic modulators (AOMs) within the OPLL synchronization scheme. Peng et al. \cite{peng} demonstrated that by maliciously altering the wavelength of the reference light, an eavesdropper systematically shifts the operational parameters of the intensity modulators. This subtle alteration causes the modulators to generate quantum states with a distinctly higher mean photon number than intended. Empirical analysis confirms that this highly localized attack can increase the mean photon number by up to $8.7\%$, fundamentally breaking the decoy-state assumptions and compromising the secret key rate if uncalibrated.

\subsection{Quantum Machine Learning and Variational Circuits}
The intersection of quantum computing and machine learning has garnered immense interest as an auditing mechanism for highly dimensional quantum networks. Early QML algorithms focused on accelerating linear algebra subroutines, such as the Quantum Support Vector Machine (QSVM) \cite{qsvm}, but required fault-tolerant, large-scale quantum computers incorporating quantum RAM (QRAM).

In the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era, research has pivoted toward Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs). VQAs rely on a hybrid quantum-classical loop: a parameterized quantum circuit (PQC) prepares a state and evaluates a cost function, while a classical optimizer updates the parameters via gradient descent. Schuld et al. \cite{schuld} and Havlicek et al. \cite{havlicek} demonstrated that Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) can be utilized as expressive quantum classifiers, effectively mapping classical data into the exponentially large Hilbert space of the qubit system. The tensor-product structure of quantum mechanics allows these models to capture complex correlations using very few trainable parameters. Furthermore, identifying true labels in linguistic and sequence-based datasets has been recognized as critical, analogous to establishing robust ground truth in QML tasks, drawing parallels to classical dataset rigorousness established by Zampieri et al. \cite{zampieri}.

However, training VQCs is highly non-trivial due to the "Barren Plateau" phenomenon identified by McClean et al. \cite{mcclean}. As the number of qubits ($n$) and the depth of the circuit increase, the variance of the cost function gradients decays exponentially (scaling as $O(1/2^n)$), making the loss landscape entirely flat and un-trainable. Successful VQC design requires shallow circuit depths, local objective functions, and careful initialization strategies.

Recent architectures have integrated VQCs into recurrent sequence models. The Quantum Long Short-Term Memory (QLSTM) \cite{qlstm} replaces the dense neural gates of a standard LSTM with VQCs. This structure is theoretically ideal for processing continuous QKD telemetry, as it maintains temporal memory while projecting individual time-step features into highly expressive quantum states.

Furthermore, the vulnerability of QML models to adversarial perturbations is an active area of study. Lu et al. \cite{lu} proved that quantum classifiers are susceptible to crafted adversarial examples. In the context of our research, this duality is critical: we must construct a QLSTM that is sufficiently expressive to detect Eve's physical perturbations, yet robust enough to generalize against non-stationary environmental noise without suffering from adversarial domain collapse.

\section{Theoretical Framework and Physical Threat Model}
To construct an effective anomaly detection system, we must first rigorously mathematically define the physical operation of a TF-QKD network and how the targeted attacks perturb the continuous telemetry stream monitored by network operators.

\subsection{TF-QKD Baseline Telemetry}
In a symmetric TF-QKD protocol, Alice and Bob prepare phase-randomized weak coherent pulses. Let the optical field sent by Alice be $E_A = \sqrt{\mu_A} e^{i(\phi_A + \theta_A)}$ and by Bob be $E_B = \sqrt{\mu_B} e^{i(\phi_B + \theta_B)}$, where $\mu$ is the intensity, $\phi$ is the information-encoding phase, and $\theta$ is the global random phase.

These pulses propagate through optical fibers with transmittances $\eta_A$ and $\eta_B$ to reach Charlie's central beam splitter. For single-photon interference to succeed, Alice and Bob must actively lock their laser frequencies and phases using a strong continuous-wave (CW) reference laser. The resulting interference dictates the count rates at Charlie's detectors $D_0$ and $D_1$.

The overall gain $Q$ and the QBER $E$ are heavily dependent on the interference visibility $V$. Under ideal, symmetric conditions ($\eta_A = \eta_B, \mu_A = \mu_B$), visibility is bounded by device imperfections. However, the theoretical visibility is given by:
\begin{equation}
V = \frac{2 \sqrt{\eta_A \eta_B \mu_A \mu_B}}{\eta_A \mu_A + \eta_B \mu_B}
\end{equation}

The QBER is fundamentally linked to the phase error $\delta \phi_{lock}$ in the stabilization loop:
\begin{equation}
E \approx \frac{1 - V \cos(\delta \phi_{lock})}{2} + E_{opt}
\end{equation}
where $E_{opt}$ encompasses baseline optical misalignment and dark counts. A standard TF-QKD system monitors a high-dimensional vector $\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ at every time step $t$, encompassing $V$, $Q$, $E$, the reference power $P_{ref}$, synchronization jitter $\tau$, and bias voltages $v_{bias}$ across multiple bases and decoy intensities.

\subsection{Mathematical Attack Signatures}
We mathematically model six distinct physical-layer attacks, meticulously translating their physical consequences into localized telemetry perturbations that machine learning models can ingest and evaluate.

The \textbf{Detector Blinding attack} fundamentally disrupts normal counting distributions. Eve injects bright light $P_{blind}$ such that $P_{blind} \gg P_{breakdown}$. This excessive flux forces the APD current $I_{APD} > I_{threshold}$, pulling the detector into a linear regime. The primary signature of this event is a massive, sustained spike in $P_{ref}$ and internal optical power monitors. Secondary to this, the statistical variance of the coincidence rate $Var(Q)$ collapses artificially, as Eve is deterministically firing the detectors rather than relying on the natural Poisson-distributed arrival of single photons.

\textbf{Trojan-Horse attacks} via reference light tampering rely on precise optical probes. Eve probes Alice's phase modulator with short pulses $E_{probe}$. To avoid the watchdog detector threshold $P_{watch}$, Eve keeps $\int E_{probe}(t) dt < P_{watch}$. However, the injection and reflection of these pulses subtly perturb the local cavity. The distinct mathematical signature manifests as high-frequency micro-spikes in the active phase-lock error $\delta \phi_{lock}(t)$. This creates a highly non-linear, temporal anomaly in the QBER tracking that static classical thresholding fails to reliably flag.

The \textbf{Wavelength Switching attack} exploits the chromatic physics of optical fibers. Eve attempts to shift the operational wavelength from $\lambda_0$ to $\lambda' = \lambda_0 + \delta \lambda$. Optical fibers exhibit chromatic dispersion $D$ (typically $\sim 17 \text{ ps/(nm}\cdot\text{km)}$ at 1550 nm). The physical signature of this manipulation is that the pulse arrival time shifts by $\Delta \tau = D \cdot L \cdot \delta \lambda$. This introduces a steady, accumulating drift in the synchronization jitter $\tau_{sync}$ telemetry, which progressively forces the QBER to elevate as pulses partially miss the rigid detection gate windows.

\textbf{Asymmetric Loss attacks} mimic physical intercept-resend conditions. Eve performs an attack exclusively on Alice's transmission arm, causing the channel transmittances to skew drastically ($\eta_A \ll \eta_B$). According to the visibility equation, the interference visibility $V$ drops significantly due to the geometric imbalance. The baseline coincidence rates shift entirely. In statistical terms, this is a severe domain shift; the underlying amplitude of the data changes, which heavily disrupts standard classical machine learning models optimized purely on symmetric training data.

To ensure models learn actual adversarial intent rather than merely memorizing standard hardware noise, we simulate non-adversarial \textbf{Environmental Phase Drift}. Thermal fluctuations naturally cause the refractive index $n$ and length $L$ of the fiber to change, altering the phase $\phi(t) = \frac{2\pi n L}{\lambda}$. This is mathematically modeled as an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck stochastic process, generating heavy, non-stationary low-frequency noise across the QBER and Coincidence channels.

\section{Methodology: Hybrid QLSTM Architecture and Classical Baselines}
To contextualize the performance of the proposed quantum architecture, we simultaneously benchmark it against robust classical paradigms to evaluate relative expressibility, parameter efficiency, and structural resistance to overfitting.

\subsection{Classical Architectural Baselines}
The \textbf{Transformer Network} is chosen to represent the highly-parameterized classical maximum for deep sequence processing. Configured with multiple multi-head attention layers and positional encoding, it processes the entire sequence of 32 time-steps simultaneously. It computes complex attention weights directly across the 384 physical features, mathematically weighing the causal interactions, such as prioritizing the relationship between reference wavelength drift and degraded interference visibility.

The \textbf{Classical LSTM} represents a standard Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) architecture. It maps sequential dependencies in the QKD telemetry utilizing long short-term memory cells. It manages information flow through four distinct dense neural matrices: the forget gate ($f_t$), input gate ($i_t$), cell state update ($\tilde{c}_t$), and output gate ($o_t$). Each standard gate applies an affine transformation $W \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$ followed by a classical non-linear activation.

The \textbf{Autoencoder} operates strictly as an unsupervised deep learning model designed for one-class anomaly detection. Trained exclusively on the 'Normal' subset of the telemetry data, it attempts to reconstruct the signal. Anomalies and zero-day physical attacks are flagged dynamically based strictly on Mean Squared Error (MSE) reconstruction thresholding.

\subsection{Hybrid QLSTM Architecture}
To construct the Hybrid QLSTM, the classical dense affine matrices of a standard LSTM cell are entirely replaced with parameterized Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs). The governing hypothesis is that natively embedding the massive 384-dimensional telemetry sequence directly into an exponentially expansive quantum Hilbert space generates a highly expressive, non-linear feature map, allowing the model to trace complex attack boundaries with exponentially fewer parameters than classical matrices.

\textbf{Data Encoding (State Preparation):}
At time step $t$, the system receives the classical telemetry input $\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and the previous hidden state $\mathbf{h}_{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^k$. These are concatenated into a unified vector $\mathbf{v}_t = [\mathbf{h}_{t-1}, \mathbf{x}_t]$. Because quantum rotational gates operate on a strictly bounded rotational space, $\mathbf{v}_t$ is classically scaled to the interval $[-\pi, \pi]$. The vector is mapped into an $n$-qubit quantum state using an angle embedding strategy. Assuming the system initializes in the ground state $|0\rangle^{\otimes n}$, a layer of parameterized Pauli-$Y$ rotations is applied:
\begin{equation}
|\psi_{in}(\mathbf{v}_t)\rangle = \bigotimes_{j=1}^{n} R_y(v_{t, j}) |0\rangle_j
\end{equation}
If the physical dimension of $\mathbf{v}_t$ exceeds the number of available qubits $n$, Data Re-uploading \cite{data_reuploading} is utilized, sequentially alternating encoding blocks with entangling blocks to fully inject the classical vector into the state.

\textbf{Strongly Entangling Variational Ansatz:}
To process the encoded data, a trainable unitary operator $U(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is applied. To maximize expressive capability while mitigating Barren Plateaus, a hardware-efficient strongly entangling ansatz is utilized. The circuit consists of $L$ layers. Each layer executes arbitrary single-qubit rotations followed by a ring topology of Controlled-NOT (CNOT) gates to force entanglement to propagate globally across the state vector:
\begin{equation}
U(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{l=1}^{L} \left( U_{ent} \bigotimes_{j=1}^{n} U_{rot}(\theta_{j,l}^{(x)}, \theta_{j,l}^{(y)}, \theta_{j,l}^{(z)}) \right)
\end{equation}
where $U_{rot}(\alpha, \beta, \gamma) = R_z(\gamma) R_y(\beta) R_x(\alpha)$, and $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ represents the dynamically updated classical weights.

\textbf{Quantum Measurement and Gate Update:}
After the quantum state evolves to $|\psi_{out}(\mathbf{v}_t, \boldsymbol{\theta})\rangle = U(\boldsymbol{\theta}) |\psi_{in}\rangle$, information is extracted back into the classical domain by calculating the expectation values of the Pauli-$Z$ observable ($\sigma_z$) on each qubit:
\begin{equation}
y_{t,j} = \langle \psi_{out} | \sigma_z^{(j)} | \psi_{out} \rangle
\end{equation}
This specific measurement paradigm naturally bounds the raw network output strictly to $\mathbf{y}_t \in [-1, 1]^n$. The QLSTM cell maintains four totally independent VQCs, each parameterized by distinctly separate weight sets ($\boldsymbol{\theta}_f, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i, \boldsymbol{\theta}_c, \boldsymbol{\theta}_o$). The exact cellular update logic mathematically models the standard LSTM mechanics using these quantum readouts:
\begin{align}
f_t &= \sigma \left( VQC_f(\mathbf{v}_t, \boldsymbol{\theta}_f) + \mathbf{b}_f \right) \\
i_t &= \sigma \left( VQC_i(\mathbf{v}_t, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i) + \mathbf{b}_i \right) \\
\tilde{c}_t &= \tanh \left( VQC_c(\mathbf{v}_t, \boldsymbol{\theta}_c) + \mathbf{b}_c \right) \\
c_t &= f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{c}_t \\
o_t &= \sigma \left( VQC_o(\mathbf{v}_t, \boldsymbol{\theta}_o) + \mathbf{b}_o \right) \\
h_t &= o_t \odot \tanh(c_t)
\end{align}
where $\sigma$ is the classical sigmoid function, $\odot$ represents element-wise multiplication, and $\mathbf{b}$ are classical trainable biases. The final hidden state of the sequence $\mathbf{h}_{T}$ is passed through a dense classical Softmax layer to yield the final multi-class attack probability distribution.

\textbf{Optimization via Parameter-Shift Rule:}
Unlike classical backpropagation using the chain rule, taking the exact gradient of a complex quantum circuit analytically requires natively evaluating the circuit on quantum hardware. The training loop utilizes the parameter-shift rule. For any rotational parameter $\theta_k$ within the VQC, the exact analytical gradient of the loss function $\mathcal{L}$ is computed deterministically by executing the hardware circuit twice, shifting the parameter by a macroscopic constant of $\pi/2$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_k} = \frac{\mathcal{L}(\theta_k + \pi/2) - \mathcal{L}(\theta_k - \pi/2)}{2}
\end{equation}
This mathematical technique allows the QLSTM to be integrated seamlessly into modern autodifferentiation frameworks.

\subsection{Methodological Correctness: Strict Workflow Separation}
A fundamental, mandatory methodological requirement strictly enforced throughout this entire analysis is the absolute segregation of the multiclass supervised evaluation metrics from the one-class anomaly detection metrics. Historically, security literature occasionally compares the accuracy of a supervised classifier directly against an unsupervised anomaly detector on the exact same threat matrix. This is mathematically and scientifically invalid.

A supervised model heavily benefits from deterministic, explicit mapping to known labels during its training epoch. An anomaly detector operates with absolutely zero prior mathematical knowledge of attack signatures, optimizing strictly and solely for signal reconstruction error over normal physical baselines. Therefore, the classical and quantum supervised models (Transformer, LSTM, QLSTM) are strictly evaluated via Macro-Averaged F1-Score and Accuracy. The Autoencoder anomaly pipeline is uniquely evaluated by its statistical capacity to cleanly separate the normal distribution from all aggregated attack classes utilizing binary ROC AUC (Receiver Operating Characteristic Area Under the Curve) and Average Precision, exclusively governed by a dynamically thresholded mean squared error limit.
